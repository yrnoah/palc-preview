Important Information

  • Cultural Sensitivities

    Greeting: Australians are fairly informal, so greetings are casual and relaxed. You can expect to be greeted by first name with a friendly handshake, a happy face and a warm smile. Direct eye contact will usually make a good first impression as this demonstrates confidence, respect and openness.

    Diversity: Australia is known as a multicultural society, a melting pot that encompasses nations and culture from all over the world. The common values of Australian society aim to include tolerance and understanding towards people regardless of gender, race, class, religion or ethnicity. Australians want to be treated equally and promote fairness.

    Religion: A century ago, 96% of Australians identified as Christian and religion was a prominent factor of civic life. However, things have changed significantly since then. Although Christianity remains the dominant belief system in Australia, its civic voice has faded significantly. In this century due to extensive immigration made Australia one of the most religiously diverse societies in the world. Almost all faiths are represented, with significant numbers of Muslims, Buddhists, Jews an Hindus.

    Public Nuisance: Smoking is banned in railway platforms, light rail stops, light rail stations, bus stops, taxi ranks and ferry wharves; ‘within 10 meters of children’s play equipment in outdoor public spaces’, ‘within four meters of a pedestrian access point to a public building’; Pitt Street Mall (between Market and King Streets) is permanently smoke-free.

    Public Display of Affection: It is very common to see people holding hands, hugging and sometimes kissing in public.

  • Language

    Australia’s official language is English. However, Australia is a multicultural nation with a significant migrant population, so it’s common to hear a diverse range of languages in Australia’s cities and town.

  • Weather

    End of August is the beginning of spring for Sydney. The climate is subtropical with mild, sunny weather all year-round. However, the climate is measured by the sea, so those areas that are closest to the coast will experience a milder climate throughout the year, while the areas further inland will experience the extremes of the weather.

    The average temperature for September is 14 degrees and lies between from 9 to 18 degrees throughout the day. The average rainfall is 99mm. The weather is cool in August, Sydney can experience some chilly westerly winds this month. Temperatures do drop on an evening too, so make sure to pack a jacket when you are visiting Sydney in August.

  • Money

    The national currency in Australia is Australian dollars (AUD).

    Coins: 5,10,20 and 50 cents; 1 and 2 dollars.

    Banknotes: $5, $10, $20, $50 and $100.

  • Tipping

    Hotels and restaurants do not add service charges to your bill, and tipping is always your choice. In upmarket restaurants, it is common to tip waiters 10% of the bill for good service.

  • Sales Tax

    Goods and service tax (GST) is a value added tax of 10% on most goods and services sales. You may be able to claim a refund for the GST paid on goods if you have spent AUD$300 or more with a single business, no more than 60 days before departing Australia. Tourist Refund Scheme (TRS) facilities are located in the departure area of international terminals. Learn more about TRS here: https://www.abf.gov.au/entering-and-leaving-australia/tourist-refund-scheme

  • Transportation

    Getting Around in Sydney is easy. Public Transport in Sydney is provided by an extensive network of operating modes including suburban rail, light rail, buses, ferries and taxis.

    Taxis in Sydney can be hailed from the kerb on the street or engaged at one of several designated taxi ranks throughout the city centre.

    Taxis can also be booked directly with one of numerous private taxi companies operating across the city.

    Uber is also available in Sydney – with one tap, it connects you with a reliable ride in minutes.

    Safety

    Sydney is generally a safe city by world standards, however, as with any other large city it is important to be aware of your surroundings and take reasonable precautions to stat safe.

    With a rate of 86% and is ranked 10th out of 162 on the ranking of the safest and most dangerous countries. The large city has some localised crime issues and gang violence; however, tourists are very unlikely to be targeted. There is nearly no pickpocket risk in Sydney. A few simple precautions will minimize your chances of being pickpocketed.

    Australia has an alert system for possible terrorist attacks. The threat levels range from “not expected” to “certain”. The Australian National security website has up-to-date information regarding the current terrorism threat level. You may also contact the Australian National Security Hotline at +65-1-800-123-400. Visit their website on https://www.nationalsecurity.gov.au/.
